3.1 Descriptive Statistics

The first step of the analysis was finding descriptive statistics of subsets of data. The subsets that were used are bridges in Omaha that are in poor condition along with bridges with high traffic in poor condition.

3.1.1 Descriptive Statistics: Bridges in Omaha that are in Poor Condition

Table 3.1 shows that the average year of the bridges built in Omaha, NE was 1955. This data also shows that all of the structural condition ratings are low, as the average is shockingly below 4 (out of 10). Also, the average daily traffic (ADT_029) mean is 12240 cars per day.

3.1.2 Descriptive Statistics: Bridges in Omaha that are in Poor Condition and have High Traffic

These bridges displayed in Table 3.2 are at risk of collapse and they also have the highest traffic flow among at risk bridges. These bridges are further at risk due to the fact that the mean average daily traffic is 38160 which is very high compared to most bridges in Omaha, NE. It can be shown form the descriptive statistics that the mean of the structural conditions is below 3 which is lower than if the bridges were at poor condition. It can also be seen that the average year built is slightly newer at 1969 than bridges that are not high traffic. Finally, we can see that median of the average number of lanes is 3.92 which is much higher than the previous subset. The next step is to look at the proportion of bridges in Omaha that have a poor condition and that have a poor condition under high traffic.

3.1.3 Proportion of Bridges in Poor Condition

Table 3.3 shows that the percent chance that a bridge in Omaha, NE is in poor condition is 4.96 %. Along with that, the percent chance that a bridge in poor condition has high traffic is 1.42%. This tells us that there is a minimal chance of coming across a bridge that is at risk of collapse by structural ratings. This was a solution primarily based on descriptive statistics, the next step is to develop a model.

3.2 Statistical Model and Analysis

To do this task a collapse variable was created. This will be done by creating a binary variable that depends on if the bridge condition is poor and if the structural evaluation of the bridge in less than 4. These two variables give the best insight into if a bridge is at risk of collapse, hence they will be used to create the response variable for the model. The data that also contains many “NA” values, to take care of this issue the data will be imputed, or estimated values for the “NA” ’s will be formed based on information in the data set. The new imputed data set is what will be used to form the model Little (2021).

3.2.1 Generalized Linear Model

The model used will be the following:

\[ \log\left( \frac{P(\text{collapse\_risk} = 1)}{1 - P(\text{collapse\_risk} = 1)} \right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{DECK\_COND\_058} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{OPERATING\_RATING\_064} + \beta_3 \cdot \text{YEAR\_BUILT\_027} \]

The model above is a generalized linear model, as the response variable is non - normal ( Binomial), a normal linear model can not be used. The modification is done by using the logit link function. From this model the regression coefficient estimates will be obtained to use for prediction when the next bridge will collapse. The regression coefficients will be found using a training subset of the data (80% of the rows), which will be found using the caret package. This will then allow us to test the prediction capabilities of our model using the remaining 20% of our data, the test data set.

3.2.1.1 Coefficient Estimates

Table 3.4 shows that all variables are significant by having a p.value less than .05. This means that all of the coefficients will be included in the model for prediction. The prediction capabilities of the model were tested by using the predict function in R. This will allow an understanding of how good the model is at predicting the response variable, collapse risk, in the test data set.

3.2.2 Prediction and Model Accuracy

From the model, when used in prediction on the test data set, the model predicts or analyzed that there is 2.38 % of bridges that are at risk of collapse. This is very similar to the value from the overall data which was 4.13 %. This shows the model is representative of the overall data set and the current condition, the final step is to test the accuracy of the model.

Table 3.5 shows that the model has a 95.2% accuracy in predicting the defined collapse risk variable. Which shows that it can be predicted with good accuracy if a bridge is at risk of collapse in Omaha,NE.
